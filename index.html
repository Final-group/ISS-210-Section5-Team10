About

The Earth’s temperature has risen by 0.14 degrees fahrenheit per decade since 1880, with an increase of 0.32 degrees fahrenheit since 1981 (Lindsey, Dahlman). These changes negatively affect organisms, communities, and livelihoods through increased droughts, floods, melting glaciers, and heat waves. Effects of climate change tend to be exacerbated in California, as this state already has a very limited water supply, and droughts caused by climate change worsen this issue. The significant increase in temperature affects not only animals and agriculture, but can be especially harmful to marginalized groups as well, especially in California. California has one of the fastest growing populations in the entirety of the United States, and at the same time has one of the most limited water resources. Population growth in regions that are experiencing droughts combined with water scarcity due to increased consumption increases instances of these shortages, and people with insufficient access to water tend to be members of households with lower incomes, people of color, or both (Housel). Additionally, in the past, after disasters such as Hurricane Maria, officials have dumped debris into lower-income neighborhoods. This cannot continue to happen. As these shortages and injustices continue to occur increasingly frequently, states must consider adding protections for these lower-income populations that suffer disproportionate harm under these circumstances (Housel).

In order to relieve the negative effects of climate change on marginalized groups, we must continue to have discussions and educate others on this topic. Additionally, we can sign petitions that will encourage states to create laws to protect these marginalized groups that are disproportionately affected by climate change, continue to recognize disparities, and engage with these communities to give them more political visibility. Helping put an end not just to the injustices these groups are facing, but climate change as well, should be within everyone’s universe of obligation, and we encourage others to aid in the fight to put an end to these social injustices.

Extreme California Wildfires

In this picture, we see wildfires spreading across California in 2018. In 2018, many of these wildfires were a very tragic result of extreme drought in this state. These wildfires were very unpredictable, uncontrollable, and devastating to all sorts of life. We saw many fatalities, loss of homes, buildings, properties, worth hundreds of millions of dollars of damage. These wildfires happen nearly every year, with some years being worse than others. Severe droughts are usually the cause of these wildfires and can become a serious threat to wildlife, humans, and the whole environment itself.

Why Climate Change is Anti-Justice

This video explains how marginalized communities face disproportionate effects when it comes to climate change, and how though the U.S. is viewed as a country that is “ahead of the curve” compared to other countries, we can find a lot of environmental disparities in our own states such as California. It described an instance in Miami where, after Hurricane Maria, officials dumped debris from the damages into a low-income neighborhood with a dominant population of people of color. It also reminds viewers about other instances that have occurred in other U.S. states, such as people who couldn’t afford or weren’t physically able to evacuate Hurricane Harvey and had to stay in their homes as they flooded. Additionally, rising levels of heat can be devastating for families in homes that can’t afford air conditioning. “As climate change brings the average temperature up, systemic inequalities like this will become more obvious.” The video concluded by describing laws surrounding this issue and emphasized that we have to recognize disparities and engage with communities that are disproportionately affected by this crisis. “That way, as we find solutions, everyone has a seat at the table.”

Droughts in California

In this image, we see the extreme water loss (also known as drought) in Folsom Lake near Sacramento, California. In the past, this bridge we see here, was fully submerged in water at least 40 feet deep. California’s “water year” usually begins around October 1st and ends on September 30th. This “year” can be defined as “the official 12-month timeframe used by water managers to compile and compare hydrologic records.” (Source) We see an increase in water loss during this year and an influx of heat waves throughout the state. We can also see this trend (water year) in surrounding states, such as Arizona and Nevada.

Agricultural Impacts

In this image it is visible that due to the water being so scarce the agriculture in the state of California is greatly affected. Not only farmers are affected by the lack of sales they are able to make but also the population is affected due to there being a scarcity in certain crops. The University of California has completed a new study where they came to the conclusion that the crops which are grown now in certain areas won't be able to grow in the next few years due to the drastic climate change affecting california.
